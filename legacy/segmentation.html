Network Segmentation, Vlans, & Subnetting

Implementing segmentation, VLAN assignments, and subnetting optimizes traffic flow and meets the healthcare office’s security and compliance needs.

Overview

As we transition from a basic small office setup to an enterprise-grade network, Network Segmentation is a core pillar of our security and performance strategy. By splitting our infrastructure into multiple logical segments (VLANs), we reduce the attack surface, better contain threats, and tailor resource access based on device role and service type instead of leaving everything on one flat network. This ensures that sensitive data such as Patient Health Information (PHI), internal services, public-facing systems, cameras, guest access, and management interfaces remain separated appropriately.

  • Enforces Zero-Trust principles by limiting default trust between subnets.
  • Simplifies Access Control with refined ACLs and firewall rules.
  • Enables Scalability and modular growth, accommodating new services and devices with ease.

Summit Care IP Scheme & Subnet Design

Summit Care’s segmentation model starts from a single private office address space and breaks it into functional VLANs and subnets. Rather than separating the network by department, the design separates it by role: endpoints, internal servers, web-facing systems, cameras, guest access, VPN services, and management interfaces. This gives the office a cleaner routing model and makes ACLs, firewall rules, and monitoring much easier to manage.

Overall Office Range

The office uses an overall 10.7.0.0/16 private space, which is then broken into smaller subnets for the different systems that make up the Summit Care environment.

Role-Based Subnets

Subnets are assigned by function such as desktops, internal servers, cameras, guest wireless, and management rather than by office title or department.

Security & Clarity

Because every subnet has a defined purpose, firewall ACLs, routing rules, DHCP scopes, and logging can be applied with much less ambiguity and lower lateral movement risk.

VLAN Architecture & Design

Our deployment utilizes Cisco switching, Cisco ASA/firewall controls, and RADIUS-based authentication to support a function-based VLAN model. Each VLAN is aligned to a service role inside the Summit Care environment:

VLAN ID Network Role IP Range Purpose
10 User Endpoints 10.7.10.0/24 Office desktops and standard staff laptops
20 Internal Servers 10.7.20.0/24 Active Directory, DNS, DHCP, NPS/RADIUS, infrastructure VMs
30 Web & Public Services 10.7.30.0/24 Web-facing utilities and externally reachable support services
40 Database & Application Data 10.7.40.0/24 Practice database and protected backend application traffic
50 Guest Wi-Fi 10.7.50.0/24 Internet-only guest wireless with no internal resource visibility
60 Cameras 10.7.60.0/24 Ubiquiti camera systems and isolated surveillance devices
70 VPN & Remote Access 10.7.70.0/24 Remote staff connectivity and VPN-related services
80 Voice 10.7.80.0/24 Teams phones, voice endpoints, and telephony support traffic
90 Management 10.7.90.0/24 Switch, firewall, router, NAS, printer, and management IP access
99 Out-of-Band / iDRAC 10.7.99.0/24 Dedicated management for server recovery interfaces such as iDRAC
  • Database, server, and web-facing services are separated so internal systems do not sit on the same network as exposed resources.
  • Guest Wi-Fi is isolated on its own network with no internal resource visibility.
  • Camera systems and management interfaces remain off the general endpoint network to reduce unnecessary exposure.

Security & Access Controls

VLAN segmentation is only as robust as the policies and rules that govern inter-VLAN traffic. For Summit Care, segmentation is paired with a layered access control model using Cisco firewalls, Windows Server firewalling, pfSense-style filtering, and ACL policies:

ACL Policies

Each VLAN-to-VLAN communication path is explicitly defined in the Cisco ASA firewall or routed policy layer. By default, deny all is enforced, and only required traffic is allowed between networks.

RADIUS Authentication

Wireless clients authenticate through the Windows RADIUS/NPS service, with certificate-based access where supported and username/password-backed enterprise wireless policies where required.

IPS/IDS Integration

Intrusion Prevention/Detection Systems and monitored firewall controls are placed at key network junctions so suspicious traffic between internal, public, and management networks can be detected and investigated quickly.

Policy Audits

Routine HIPAA compliance checks confirm that ACLs, logs, Windows firewall settings, and network flow data match the clinic’s security and privacy expectations.

Trunking & Inter-VLAN Routing

Our Cisco switching uses 802.1Q trunking to forward multiple VLANs across shared links, while the routing and policy layer controls how traffic moves between those networks. Summit Care’s configuration workflow also includes SSH-based administration, saved exports, and documented configuration records:

  • Trunk Ports carry the VLAN tags needed between Cisco switches, access layers, and routing points.
  • Inter-VLAN Routing is handled through the routed/firewall layer so traffic can be filtered before moving across networks.
  • Administrative Changes are made through SSH, documented, and backed up through saved configuration exports and downloaded records.

Sample Subnet Allocation

Below is a simplified IP plan reflecting how Summit Care allocates addresses across the environment. DHCP scopes are supported where appropriate, while critical systems such as servers, switches, firewalls, NAS services, printers, cameras, and management interfaces use static or documented reserved addressing.

VLAN 10 (User Endpoints):
10.7.10.0/24 endpoint pool
VLAN 20 (Internal Servers):
10.7.20.0/24 server services
VLAN 30 (Web & Public Services):
10.7.30.0/24 public-facing zone
VLAN 40 (Database & App Data):
10.7.40.0/24 protected data tier
VLAN 50 (Guest Wi-Fi):
10.7.50.0/24 isolated guest pool
VLAN 60 (Cameras):
10.7.60.0/24 camera network
VLAN 70 (VPN Services):
10.7.70.0/24 remote access pool
VLAN 90 / 99 (Management & iDRAC):
10.7.90.0/24 + 10.7.99.0/24 management

Dynamic leases are set for end-user devices where appropriate, while static or reserved addresses are used for the Cisco ASA firewall, Cisco switches, routers, Windows domain services, Synology NAS, printer, Ubiquiti cameras, and the server’s iDRAC management interface. This keeps management access predictable and easier to document, secure, and troubleshoot.

Monitoring & Compliance Checks

Maintaining strong segmentation is an ongoing process. Summit Care tracks VLAN behavior, management access, and routing boundaries through logging, SNMP monitoring, and configuration review so security rules do not drift away from the documented design.

Real-Time Traffic Analysis

Network flow data and interface behavior can be reviewed to understand VLAN utilization, unusual routing patterns, and capacity concerns.

Unauthorized Access Alerts

If a device appears on an unexpected subnet, management path, or guest network, alerts and log review support faster investigation.

HIPAA Audit Trails

ACL changes, segmentation records, and management actions are documented and reviewed so the clinic can support both operational clarity and HIPAA-aware audit expectations.

Conclusion & Next Steps

By organizing Summit Care’s network around role-based VLANs and subnets, documenting the IP scheme, separating management traffic, and tying inter-VLAN communication back to firewall ACLs and secure administration, the clinic now has a much clearer and safer internal network structure. The next step is to continue into Disaster Recovery & Backup Plan, where this segmented design supports recovery readiness, backup scope, and restoration planning across the environment.
